A reasonable name for the less than and greater than signs

Proposed Names: Let and Gret

It is proposed that names be given to the signs for the "less than symbol" (<) and the "greater than symbol" (>).

We here propose that the "<" be named a "let" - arising from the "le" and "t" in "less than" and "gret" arising similarly from the "gr" and "t" in "greater than" names.

The Need for a Name for a Symbol

As rational creatures with a natural ability for symbolic ideas and communication, we humans can and should use terms to more quickly converse about the things of which we speak.

Take for example the "less than" (<) and "greater than" (>) symbols - which themselves are abstractions of at least two ideas. The first use is normally learned in gradeschool or the like and represents a way to compare the value of two quantities: 5<10. x>-3. A second use is as an organizational structure in various coding situations, including most ubiquitously in the hyper text markup language (html). "<p>" for example signifies the start of a new paragraph on a webpage. The use of the less than and greater than are not to signify a difference in value but to organize tags that separate different content pieces in the html document.

The need for names of "<" and ">" in practice for both of the situations is not highly necessary, but as a pedagogical tool the frequent reference gives a functional need for specific terms to be used.

Situation where the Need is Aparent

In the classroom setting teaching html, a student often may forget to include the let or gret in their markup. Student S may type "<a href=index.html Click here>". The correct typing would look more like this: "<a href=index.html> Click here</a>". An instructor could quickly communicate to the student that they need an extra gret after the index.html as well as a let and a gret around the /a to form the closing tag "</a>". Giving these terms serves at least two purposes:

  1. Allows the two individuals to more quickly communicate about the different pieces as needed
  2. Having a term may further enable the student to create an abstraction of the concept and give them more faculty to understand the purpose of the symbol in this context

The second purpose in particular is an essential aspect of the learning process - that is, giving an individual the ability to masterfully (read - as a teacher does) use a concept for their benefit and the benefit of another. Students tend to better apply a concept and rules when they understand the logic and reasoning for those rules. This naming proposal of the let and gret is an example in situ of education where a term given to an abstract concept empowers the learner to understand the concept and apply it more readily.

Other Current Examples

  1. The let and gret are used in php as it is also intertwined with html.
  2. The let and the gret are used for wrapping around a generics (for example <E>).
  3. The bitwise operators in a number of languages use two lets and grets successively.

Closing Remarks

The author does not contend that this is the best naming convention or that it will significanlty increase the ability for a student to learn and discuss these concepts. However, in practice, the author has noticed a quicker grasp of the concept by students.
